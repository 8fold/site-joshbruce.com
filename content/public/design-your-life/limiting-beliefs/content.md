# Limiting beliefs and the fear of rework

As the name suggests, limiting beliefs are those things we believe that limit possibilities.

One of the most debilitating is the idea that there is one best (or right) solution and everything else is bad (or wrong). This can lead to a belief that if I need to revisit something in the future, it’s because I didn’t get it right the first time.

“I only want to do it once,” is a catchphrase one might hear from someone stuck here. Or, when asked when something will be done, the reply is often, “It’s  almost ready” or “It’s so close.”

For better and worse, that’s not how any of this works.

I cannot tell you the number of times I’ve had to clean my kitchen. And why do I have to update my operating system every year? 

It’s difficult to accept (and possibly embrace) impermanence.

In software development (or any such creative field) testers might complain that the code their testing keeps changing before they can finish. Coders might complain that the designs aren’t “final” or keep changing. Visual designers might complain that user research keeps revealing new insights that change the visual design. User research folks might complain that they keep finding new users to talk to, resulting in more insights.

Accepting impermanence often becomes harder the more time and emotional labor there is invested in a thing.

This tendency can be the reason folks stay in bad relationships. And this covers all relationships. “Oh, couldn’t leave, we’ve been married X years and there’s the house, kids, dog, plants (and so on).” Or, in a job, “I can’t quit, I’ve been here X years, I like some of the people I work with, I’m so close to retirement (and so on).”

It’s important to note that this can look like analysis paralysis, however, they aren’t the same.

Analysis paralysis often occurs due to the paradox of choice. Out of all these options available I should be able to find the perfect one. What we’re talking about here is more akin to perfectionism.

The quest for the idealistic “final” version.

The blunt of it:

> Final doesn’t exist and everything gets thrown away.
