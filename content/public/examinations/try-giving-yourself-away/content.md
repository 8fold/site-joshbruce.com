# Try Giving Yourself Away

*Try Giving Yourself Away* is a 132 page book, available for free (as of this writing) at [the internet archive](https://archive.org/details/trygivingyoursel031988mbp/page/n15/mode/2up). It's an expansion of an article that was sent anonymously and published in Forbes Magazine and was reprinted in an event more condensed form in Reader's Digest. 

The introduction contains the following observation:

> These are troubled times. The world is full of strife and heartache. Men and women everywhere seek peace of mind and heart, and wish desperately that they as individuals could do something toward lifting the heavy blanket of gloom and fear that oppresses mankind.

We can call this the problem statement and goes on to prepare us for what the author hopes we'll find in the pages of the book as well as a call to action:

> Perhaps you and I can help. More than anything else, the world needs the healing influence of a great surge of simple kindheartedness, to rid humanity of jealousy, selfishness and greed. Such a surge must start with us, as individuals; it is beyond the power of the world's rulers or statesmen. In our daily living we average citizens must establish the spirit and set the pattern of a kindlier world.
>
> I invite you to join me in my hobby.

Chapter one describes how David Dunn came upon this possible hobby of giving yourself away. Dunn was riding a train, the Twentieth Century Limited, eastbound from Chicago to New York. As he was falling asleep he wondered idly where the eastbound and westbound trains passed and thought that might be an interesting subject for some of the advertisements for the railroad. The next morning he wrote a letter to the railroad explaining the "Where the Centuries Pass" idea with, "no string attached." 

A few days later Dunn received acknowledgment of the letter. A few months later he received a letter informing him that the idea was the inspiration for the 1924 New York Central calendar. Dunn goes on to describe the image:

> a night picture of the oncoming locomotive of one Century and the lighted observation platform of the other, passing on a curve. It was a scene rich in color and railroad romance.

Dunn says he traveled extensively that summer and how he would see "his" Century calendar in almost every railroad station and hotel lobby. It filled him with a glow knowing he had contributed in the making in some small way.

Dunn opens the chapter with the timeless cynicism that:

> Like most people, I was brought up to look upon life as a process of *getting*.

Coming off the back of the story of giving away the idea that would later be used as inspiration for the calendar, Dunn took up his hobby of giving-away; noting that successful giving-away must be cultivated. Dunn described himself as a collector, a collector of glows and after-glows. In the parlance of our times, we might say Dunn collected experiences and those experiences were directly related to him *giving* not *getting* (at least not getting beyond the experience, glow, and memory).
